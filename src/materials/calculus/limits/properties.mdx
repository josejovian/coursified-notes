# Properties of Limits

<Theorem title="Calculating Limits">
This page discusses the properties of limits. To summarize this entire page, to calculate a limit, you can think of it just simply subtituting $x$ into $f(x)$ to find the limit. However, there will be exceptions that will be discussed later.
</Theorem>

---

<Formula>
$\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} c = c$
</Formula>

**The limit of a constant function is the constant itself.** At any point $x$, the function will be always equal to the constant, so finding the limit of this function as $x$ approaches any point would result the same, that is, the constant itself.

---

<Formula>
$\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} x = a$
</Formula>

Similar to the previous one, this is self-explanatory.

---

<Formula>
$\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} c \times f(x) = c \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x)$
</Formula>

The limit of a function **multiplied by a constant** is equal to the constant multiplied by the limit of a function.

---

<Formula>
$\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} \left [f(x) \pm g(x) \right] =\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x) \pm \lim\limits\_{x\rightarrow a} g(x) $
</Formula>

The **sum or difference** of the limit of two functions is equal when **adding or subtracting** them individually.

---

<Formula>
$\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x)g(x) =\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x) \times \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} g(x)$  
 &nbsp;
$\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \large \frac{\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x)}{\lim\limits\_{x\rightarrow a} g(x)}, \normalsize \text{where } g(x) \neq 0 $
</Formula>

The **product or quotient** of the limit of two functions is equal when **multiplying or dividing** them individually.

---

<Formula>
$\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} \left [ f(x) \right ]^n = \left [ \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x) \right ]^n$
</Formula>

Given that $n$ is any real number, including fractions, the limit of **(the function $f$ to the power of $n$)** is equal to **(the limit of the function $f$)** to the power of $n$. Due to the previous law or property, this one should be self-explanatory.

# Calculating Limits: Direct Substitution

<Example>
Evaluate the following limits:
1.  $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 4} \left [ 4x^2 - 4x + 1 \right ] = ...$
2.  $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow -2} \frac{x^2 - 1} {9x^2 + 6x + 3}  = ...$
3.  $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \left (\frac{x-3}{x^2} \right ) = ...$
4.  $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{x^2 + 1}  = ...$
</Example>

**Limit 1**  
$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow 4} \left [ 4x^2 - 4x + 1 \right ] &= \lim_{x\rightarrow 4} 4x^2 - \lim_{x\rightarrow 4} 4x + \lim_{x\rightarrow 4} 1\\
 &= 64 - 16 + 1\\
 &= 49\\
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 2**  
$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow -2} \frac{x^2 - 1}{9x^2 + 6x + 3} &= \lim_{x\rightarrow -2} \frac{(-2)^2 - 1}{(9 \times (-2)^2) + (6 \times (-2)) + 3}\\
 &= \frac{3}{27}\\
 &= \frac{1}{9}\\
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 3**  
Here, we encounter a division by zero. However, we need to know that $\lim_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{x^2} = \infty$.
$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow 0} \left (\frac{x-3}{x^2}\right )
 &= \left (\lim_{x\rightarrow 0} \left [ x-3 \right ] \right )\left (\lim_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{x^2} \right )\\
 &= (-3)(\infty)\\
 &= -\infty
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 4**  
$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{x^2 + 1}
 &=\frac{1}{\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left [ x^2 + 1 \right ]}\\
 &=\frac{1}{\infty}\\
 &=0
\end{align*}
$$

# Calculating Limits: Alternative Methods

<Example>
Evaluate the following limits:
1.  $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{x^2-5x+4}{x^2-1} = ...$
2.  $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{x^5-x^3} {x^6-2x^3}  = ...$
3.  $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}-\sqrt{ax^2+qx+r} = ...$
4.  $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{2x-\sqrt{x + 3}}{x - 1}  = ...$
</Example>

As you have noticed, direct substitution does not work at all here. However, there are methods that we can use.

**Limit 1: Factorize**  
Directly subtituting yields the indeterminate form $\frac{0}{0}$. Factorize both numerator and denominator, then remove the common factor. 
$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{x^2-5x+4}{x^2-1} &= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1}  \frac{(x-1)(x-4)}{(x-1)(x+1)}\\
 &= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{x-4}{x+1}\\
 &= -\frac{3}{2}\\
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 2: Divide to the Largest Power**  
Directly substituting yields multiple indeterminate forms. Since this is a limit where both numerator and denominator are polynomials, as $x$ approaches infinity, divide both sides with x to the highest power.
$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{x^5-x^3} {x^6-2x^3} &= \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{x^5-x^3} {x^6-2x^3} \times \frac{\frac{1}{x^6}}{\frac{1}{x^6}}\\
 &= \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{\frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{x^3}}{1-\frac{2}{x^3}}\\
 &= \frac{0 - 0}{1 - 0}\\
&= 0\\
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 3: Rationalize and Divide to the Largest Power**  
Keep in mind that the only variable here is $x$, whereas the other letters are all constants. Directly substituting yields the indeterminate form $\infty - \infty$. Rationalize it.
$$
\begin{align*}
&\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}-\sqrt{ax^2+qx+r}\\
&=\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}-\sqrt{ax^2+qx+r}\times \frac{\sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}+\sqrt{ax^2+qx+r}}{\sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}+\sqrt{ax^2+qx+r}}\\
&=\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{(ax^2+bx+c)-(ax^2+qx+r)}{\sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}+\sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}}\\
&=\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{(b-q)x +(c-r)}{\sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}+\sqrt{ax^2+qx+r}}\\
&=\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{(b-q)x +(c-r)}{\sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}+\sqrt{ax^2+qx+r}} \times \frac{\frac{1}{x}}{\frac{1}{x}}\\
&=\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{(b-q) + \frac{c-r}{x}}{\sqrt{a+\frac{b}{x}+\frac{c}{x^2}}+\sqrt{a+\frac{b}{x}+\frac{c}{x^2}}}\\
&=\frac{(b-q) + 0}{\sqrt{a+0+0}+\sqrt{a+0+0}}\\
&=\frac{b-q}{2\sqrt{a}}
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 4: Rationalize & Factorize**  
Direct substitution yields the indeterminate form $\frac{0}{0}$. 
$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{2x-\sqrt{x + 3}}{x - 1}
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{2x-\sqrt{x + 3}}{x - 1} \times \frac{2x+\sqrt{x + 3}}{2x+\sqrt{x + 3}}\\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{4x^2-x-3}{(x-1)(2x+\sqrt{x + 3})}\\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{(x-1)(4x+3)}{(x-1)(2x+\sqrt{x + 3})}\\
&= \frac{7}{4}\\
\end{align*}
$$

Multiple methods may be required to solve a limit, as shown above.

# **Practice:** Calculating Limits

1. Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 4} \frac{8 - \sqrt{x^3}}{\sqrt{x} - 2}=...$

<Practice id="calculating-limits-1" answer="-12" indent />

<Explanation>
Let $v = \sqrt{x}$. Since we're converting from one to another variable, the $x$ we're approaching in the limit also changes accordingly. Even if you do not subsitute, **the result will stay the same**.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow 4} \frac{8 - \sqrt{x^3}}{\sqrt{x} - 2} &= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} \frac{8 - v^3}{v - 2} \\
&= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} \frac{- (v^3 - 8)}{v - 2} \\
&= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} \frac{- (v - 2)(v^2 + 2v + 4)}{v - 2} \\
&= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} -(v^2 + 2v + 4) \\
&= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} -(2^2 + 2\times2 + 4) \\
&= -12 \\
\end{align*}
$$
</Explanation>

2. Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty }(\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}-3x-4)=...$

<Practice id="calculating-limits-2" answer="-3" indent />

<Explanation>
$$
\begin{align*}
&\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} [\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}-3x-4]\\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} [\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}-(3x+4)] \\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} [\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}-\sqrt{(3x+4)^2}] \\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} [\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}-\sqrt{9x^2+24x+16}] \\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} [\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}-\sqrt{9x^2+24x+16}] \times \frac{[\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}+\sqrt{9x^2+24x+16}]}{[\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}+\sqrt{9x^2+24x+16}]}  \\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{(9x^2+6x-3) - (9x^2+24x+16)}{\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}+\sqrt{9x^2+24x+16}} \\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{(-18x - 19)}{\sqrt{9x^2+6x-3}+\sqrt{9x^2+24x+16}} \\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{(-18 - \frac{19}{x})}{\sqrt{9+\frac{6}{x}-\frac{3}{x^2}}+\sqrt{9+\frac{24}{x}+\frac{16}{x^2}}} \\
&= \frac{-18}{6} \\
&= -3 \\
\end{align*}
$$
</Explanation>

3. Consider $f(x) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{4}x^{2}+16x+4}$ and $g(x) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{4}x^{2}+16x-4}$. If both $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} f(x)$ and $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} g(x)$ exists, and also $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \left [ f(x) + g(x) \right ] = 8$. Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \left [ f(x) - g(x) \right ] = ...$  

<Practice id="calculating-limits-3" answer="1" indent />

<Explanation>
$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \left [ f(x) + g(x) \right ] &= 8\\
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \left [ f(x) + g(x) \right ] \times \left ( \frac{f(x) - g(x)}{f(x) - g(x)} \right ) &= 8\\
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \frac{(f(x))^2 - (g(x))^2}{f(x) - g(x)} &= 8\\
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \frac{(\frac{1}{4}x^{2}+16x+4) - (\frac{1}{4}x^{2}+16x-4)}{f(x) - g(x)} &= 8\\
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \frac{8}{f(x) - g(x)} &= 8\\
\frac{1}{\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \frac{8}{f(x) - g(x)}} &= \frac{1}{8}\\
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \frac{f(x)-g(x)}{8} &= \frac{1}{8}\\
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow c} \left [ f(x) - g(x) \right ] &= 1
\end{align*}
$$
</Explanation>
