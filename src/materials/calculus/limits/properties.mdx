# Properties of Limits

> #formula# $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} c = c$

**The limit of a constant function is the constant itself.** At any point $x$, the function will be always equal to the constant, so finding the limit of this function as $x$ approaches any point would result the same, that is, the constant itself.

---

> #formula# $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} x = a$

Similar to the previous one, this is self-explanatory.

---

> #formula# $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} c \times f(x) = c \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x)$

The limit of a function **multiplied by a constant** is equal to the constant multiplied by the limit of a function.

---

> #formula# $\lim\limits*{x\rightarrow a} \left [f(x) \pm g(x) \right] =\lim\limits*{x\rightarrow a} f(x) \pm \lim\limits\_{x\rightarrow a} g(x) $

The **sum or difference** of the limit of two functions is equal when **adding or subtracting** them individually.

---

> #formula# $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x)g(x) =\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x) \times \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} g(x)$  
> &nbsp;
> $\lim\limits*{x\rightarrow a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \large \frac{\lim\limits*{x\rightarrow a} f(x)}{\lim\limits\_{x\rightarrow a} g(x)}, \normalsize \text{where } g(x) \neq 0 $

The **product or quotient** of the limit of two functions is equal when **multiplying or dividing** them individually.

---

> #formula# $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} \left [ f(x) \right ]^n = \left [ \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x) \right ]^n$

Given that $n$ is any real number, including fractions, the limit of **(the function $f$ to the power of $n$)** is equal to **(the limit of the function $f$)** to the power of $n$. Due to the previous law or property, this one should be self-explanatory.

# Properties of Limits: Easy Examples

> #example# **Example**  
> Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 4} \left [ 4x^2 - 4x + 1 \right ] = ...$

To simplify things, we can first think of this function as a combination of three different functions, then find the limit of each function, then apply the addition or subtraction like the following.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow 4} \left [ 4x^2 - 4x + 1 \right ] &= \lim_{x\rightarrow 4} 4x^2 - \lim_{x\rightarrow 4} 4x + \lim_{x\rightarrow 4} 1\\
 &= 64 - 16 + 1\\
 &= 49\\
\end{align*}
$$

You may have realized that it is faster to think of this as substituting $x$ into $f(x)$. However, be warned that this is not **always the case**.

---

> #example# **Example**  
> Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow -2} \frac{x^2 - 1} {9x^2 + 6x + 3}  = ...$

Again, by thinking of this as doing a simple algebra, substituting $x$ into the function to find the value:

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow -2} \frac{x^2 - 1}{9x^2 + 6x + 3} &= \lim_{x\rightarrow -2} \frac{(-2)^2 - 1}{(9 \times (-2)^2) + (6 \times (-2)) + 3}\\
 &= \frac{3}{27}\\
 &= \frac{1}{9}\\
\end{align*}
$$

# Properties of Limits: Tricky Example #1

> #example# **Example**  
> Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{x^2-5x+4}{x^2-1}  = ...$

When you plug in $x=1$ into the function, you get...

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{x^2-5x+4}{x^2-1} &= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{1-5+4}{1-1}\\
 &= \frac{0}{0}\\
\end{align*}
$$

...this! Well, this is not how we are supposed to solve this. Instead, this just means that there is a common factor between the numerator and denominator that we can cancel from both sides. In this case, it is $(x - 1)$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{x^2-5x+4}{x^2-1} &= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{(x-1)(x-4)}{(x-1)(x+1)}\\
 &= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{(x-4)}{(x+1)}\\
  &= -\frac{3}{2}\\
\end{align*}
$$

Now, we have learnt that **factorization** is one of the method that we can use when we encounter an indeterminate form $\frac{0}{0}$.

# Properties of Limits: Tricky Example #2

> #example# **Example**  
> Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \sqrt{x^2+6x-9}-\sqrt{x^2-8x+16}  = ...$

Putting $x=\infty$ will yield an indeterminate form $\infty - \infty$. However, what if there is a way for us to manipulate the equation to avoid getting indeterminate forms? Let's try **rationalizing** it.

$$
\begin{align*}
&\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \sqrt{x^2+6x-9}-\sqrt{x^2-8x+16}\\
&=\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \sqrt{x^2+6x-9}-\sqrt{x^2-8x+16}\times \frac{\sqrt{x^2+6x-9}+\sqrt{x^2-8x+16}}{\sqrt{x^2+6x-9}+\sqrt{x^2-8x+16}}\\
&=\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{(x^2+6x-9)-(x^2-8x+16)}{\sqrt{x^2+6x-9}+\sqrt{x^2-8x+16}}\\
&=\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{14x-25}{\sqrt{x^2+6x-9}+\sqrt{x^2-8x+16}}\\
&=\lim_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{14-\frac{25}{x}}{\sqrt{1+\frac{6}{x}-\frac{9}{x^2}}+\sqrt{1-\frac{8}{x}+\frac{16}{x^2}}}\\
&=\frac{14-0}{\sqrt{1+0-0}+\sqrt{1-0+0}}\\
&=7
\end{align*}
$$

You may have found one step rather confusing, that is, when we **divide both numerator and denominator with $x$**. The reason we do this is that we're dealing with $\infty$. How would we exactly calculate $\frac{\infty - 25}{\infty + \infty}$, if we were to immediately substitute the $x$ value? That is why, we need a trick like the above, so that we do not reach an indeterminate form.

Dividing both sides with $x$ for this specific case will help us evaluate the limit, because we know that $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} c \times f(x) = c \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} f(x)$ and $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$. Combining these both properties, we know that we will end up with $\frac{14}{2}$.

We can see that once we rationalize it, we can finally avoid getting indeterminate forms, Now, we have learnt that **rationalization** is one of the method that we can use to combat against indeterminate form $\infty - \infty$.

# Properties of Limits: Tricky Example #3

> #example# **Example**  
> Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{2x-\sqrt{x + 3}}{x - 1}  = ...$

Once again, putting $x=1$ will yield $\frac{0}{0}$. However, trying to factor this directly may be not a simple task. However, let us try **rationalizing** the numerator.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{2x-\sqrt{x + 3}}{x - 1}
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{2x-\sqrt{x + 3}}{x - 1} \times \frac{2x+\sqrt{x + 3}}{2x+\sqrt{x + 3}}\\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{4x^2-x-3}{(x-1)(2x+\sqrt{x + 3})}\\
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{(x-1)(4x+3)}{(x-1)(2x+\sqrt{x + 3})}\\
&= \frac{7}{4}\\
\end{align*}
$$

We can see that once we rationalize the numerator, we are fortunate enough to see a common factor, that we can cancel out. This is an unqiue example that we solved using more than one tricks.

# Piecewise Function: An Example

> #example# **Example**  
> Given $f(x) = \begin{cases} x + 4 & \text{ if } x < -4 \\ x^2 + 5x + 4 & \text{ if } x \geq -4  \end{cases}$  
> Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow -4} f(x)$

Remember the definition of limit? The limit from the left and right side at a point must be equal, for a function to have a limit at that point. Let's calculate the left side. Since we're approaching from the left side, it means that $x < -4$, so we are using $f(x)$ when it is $x < -4$:

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{x\rightarrow -4^-} f(x)
&= \lim_{x\rightarrow -4^-} x + 4\\
&= 0\\
\end{align*}
$$

Now, to the right side:

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow -4^+} f(x)
&= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow -4^+} x^2+5x+4\\
&= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow -4^+} (-4)^2+(5\times (-4))-4\\
&= 0\\
\end{align*}
$$

Since both sides are equal, we can conclude that:

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow -4} f(x)
&= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow -4^-} f(x) = \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow -4^+} f(x)\\
&= 0\\
\end{align*}
$$

We can also see that if one of the limits had different values, then $f(x)$ would not have a limit at that point ($x = -4$).

> #explanation# **Correct!** This is just another example of limit that we can solve with factorization. Tbe square root might get in our way, but let us create a new variable $v$, such that $v = \sqrt{x}$. Since we're converting from one to another variable, the $x$ we're approaching in the limit also changes accordingly. Even if you do not subsitute, **the result will stay the same**.
>
> $$
> \begin{align*}
> \lim_{x\rightarrow 4} \frac{8 - \sqrt{x^3}}{\sqrt{x} - 2} &= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} \frac{8 - v^3}{v - 2} \\
> &= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} \frac{- (v^3 - 8)}{v - 2} \\
> &= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} \frac{- (v - 2)(v^2 + 2v + 4)}{v - 2} \\
> &= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} -(v^2 + 2v + 4) \\
> &= \lim_{v\rightarrow 2} -(2^2 + 2\times2 + 4) \\
> &= -12 \\
> \end{align*}
> $$
