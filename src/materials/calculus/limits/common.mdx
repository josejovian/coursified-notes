# Limits of Trigonometric Functions

<Formula>
Given the constants $a$, and $b$:

$$
\begin{align*}
\text{1. }&\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{\sin{ax}}{bx}=\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{ax}{\sin{bx}}=\frac{a}{b}\\
\text{2. }&\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{\tan{ax}}{bx}=\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{ax}{\tan{bx}}=\frac{a}{b}\\
\text{3. }&\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{\sin{ax}}{\tan{bx}}=\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{\tan{ax}}{\sin{bx}}=\frac{a}{b}\\
\text{4. }&\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{\sin{ax}}{\sin{bx}}=\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{\tan{ax}}{\tan{bx}}=\frac{a}{b}\\ \end{align*}
$$

</Formula>

When calculating limits of functions that contain trigonometric functions, it is useful to recall basic trigonometric identities.

# Limits of Trigonometric Functions: Example

<Example>
Evaluate the following limits:
1. $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{3\sin{4x}}{4\tan{3x}} = ...$
2. $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} x \cot {x} = ...$
3. $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{1-\cos{ax}}{bx}  = ...$
4. $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{1-x}{\sin{\pi x}} = ...$
5. $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{\left( 4\sin{2x}+\tan{x}\right)}{3\sin3x} = ...$
</Example>

**Limit 1**

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{3\sin{4x}}{4\tan{3x}} &= \frac{3}{4} \times \left ( \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{\sin{4x}}{\tan{3x}} \right )\\
 &= \frac{3}{4} \times \left ( \frac{4}{3} \right )\\
 &= 1\\
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 2**

Recall that $\cot{x} = \frac{\cos{x}}{\sin{x}}$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} x \cot{x} &= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{x \cos{x}}{\sin{x}}\\
 &= \left (  \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \cos{x} \right ) \left ( \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{x}{\sin{x}} \right )\\
 &= 1\\
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 3**

Recall that $\sin^2{x} + \cos^2{x} = 1$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{1-\cos{ax}}{bx}  &= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{1-\cos{ax}}{bx} \times \frac{1 + \cos{ax}}{1 + \cos{ax}}\\
 &= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{1 - \cos^2{ax}}{bx(1 + \cos{ax})}\\
 &= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{\sin^2{ax}}{bx(1 + \cos{ax})}\\
 &= \left (  \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{\sin{ax}}{bx} \right ) \left ( \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{\sin{ax}}{1 + \cos{ax}} \right )\\
 &= \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{0}{1}\\
 &= 0
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 4**

Recall that $\sin{x} = \sin{\pi - x}$. Let us alter the limit with a new variable $y$, such that $y = 1 - x$. In the new limit, instead of $y$ approaching $1$, now $y$ is approaching $0$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{1 - x}{\sin{\pi x}} &= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{1 - x}{\sin{\pi - \pi x}}\\
&= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 1} \frac{1 - x}{\sin{\pi (1 - x)}}\\
&= \lim\limits_{y\rightarrow 0} \frac{y}{\sin{\pi y}}\\
&= \frac{1}{\pi}\\
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 5**

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{\left(4\sin{2x}+\tan{x}\right)}{3\sin{3x}} &=
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{\left(4\sin{2x}+\tan{x}\right)}{3\sin{3x}} \times \frac{\frac{1}{x}}{\frac{1}{x}} \\
&=\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{\left(\frac{4\sin{2x}}{x}+\frac{\tan{x}}{x}\right)}{\frac{3\sin{3x}}{x}} \\
&=\frac{4 \times 2 + 1}{3 \times 3}\\
&= 1
\end{align*}
$$

# The Squeeze Theorem and Proof of a Trigonometric Limit

<Theorem title="Squeeze Theorem">
Suppose that $f(x) ≤ g(x) ≤ h(x)$ for all $x$ in an open interval containing $a$, except possibly at $a$, and $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a}f(x)= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} h(x)=L$. So, $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow a} g(x)=L$.

</Theorem>

Recall the limits of trigonometric functions that were introduced few pages ago. We will use the Squeeze Theorem to prove that
$\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{\sin{x}}{x}=1$.

![Squeeze Theorem](/materials/squeeze_theorem_proof.png "Squeeze Theorem")

First, we will refer to $\angle BOC$ as $x$ in radian, and the circle's radius as $r$. Note that $BC$ and $DA$ are perpendicular to $OA$ and $OC$. Based on this, we can see that:

- $OB$'s length = $OA$'s length = $r$
- $BC$'s length = $r \sin{x}$
- $OC$'s length = $r \cos{x}$
- $AD$'s length = $r \tan{x}$

Next, we will extract three different shapes; the triangle $BOC$, the sector $BOA$, and the triangle $DOA$. Just by looking at the visualization, we can tell that:
$$ \text{Area }BOC \leq \text{Area }BOA \leq \text{Area DOA} $$

Before continuing, we must find each of the region's area:

- $\text{Triangle } BOC = \frac{1}{2} r^2 \sin{x} \cos{x}$
- $\text{Sector } BOA = \frac{1}{2}xr^2$
- $\text{Triangle } DOA = \frac{1}{2} r^2 \tan{x}$

Now that we know each of the area, we can plug the formula in:

$$
\begin{align*}
\text{Area }BOC &\leq  \text{Area }BOA \leq \text{Area }DOA\\
\frac{1}{2} r^2 \sin{x} \cos{x} &\leq \frac{1}{2} x r^2 \leq \frac{1}{2}r^2 \tan{x} \\
\sin{x} \cos{x} &\leq x \leq \tan{x} \\
\cos{x} &\leq \frac{x}{\sin{x}} \leq \frac{1}{\cos{x}} \\
\end{align*}
$$

Next, we will take the limit of all terms:

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \cos{x} &\leq \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{\sin{x}} \leq \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{\cos{x}} \\
1 &\leq \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{x}{\sin{x}} \leq 1 \\
\end{align*}
$$

Based on **Squeeze Theorem**, we can conclude that $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{x}{\sin{x}} = 1$. Although this was not what we wanted to prove initially, we can just play with limit's properties:

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{x}{\sin{x}} &= 1\\
\frac{1}{\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \frac{x}{\sin{x}}} &= \frac{1}{1}\\
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{1}{\frac{x}{\sin{x}}} &= \frac{1}{1}\\
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{\sin{x}}{x} &= 1\\
\end{align*}
$$

There we go! We have proven that $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0}\frac{\sin{x}}{x} = 1$. So that it makes more sense, see the graph below that shows the three functions: $y=\cos{x}$ in red, $y=\frac{x}{\sin{x}}$ in blue, and $y=\frac{1}{\cos{x}}$ in purple.

# The Squeeze Theorem: Example

<Example>
Evaluate $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{\sin{x}}{x}=...$
</Example>

To do this limit, we can use the Squeeze Theorem. Firstly, consider the $\sin{x}$ function, and its range (minimum and maximum possible value), which are $-1$ and $1$ respectively. Therefore, we can write the following:
$-1 \leq \sin{x} \leq 1$. Multiply all sides with $\frac{1}{x}$, then take the limit on all sides:

$$
\begin{align*}
-1 &\leq \sin{x} \leq 1\\
-\frac{1}{x} &\leq \frac{\sin{x}}{x} \leq \frac{1}{x}\\
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} -\frac{1}{x} & \leq \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{\sin{x}}{x} \leq \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{x}\\
\end{align*}
$$

We know that $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} -\frac{1}{x} = 0$ and $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$. Therefore, by Squeeze Theorem, $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \frac{\sin{x}}{x} = 0$.

# Limit of Exponential Function

<Formula>
$\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left ( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right) ^ x = e$
</Formula>

The euler number is often defined as above. Using the above, we can quickly prove that, for a constant $k$, $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left ( 1 + \frac{k}{x} \right) ^ x = e^k$.
Let us first define a new variable $y$, such that $y = \frac{x}{k}$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty}\left ( 1 + \frac{k}{x} \right) ^ x
&= \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty}\left ( 1 + \frac{1}{\frac{x}{k}} \right) ^ x \\
&= \lim\limits_{y\rightarrow \infty}\left ( 1 + \frac{1}{y} \right) ^ {yk} \\
&= \lim\limits_{y\rightarrow \infty}\left [ \left ( 1 + \frac{1}{y} \right) ^ y \right ] ^ k \\
&= e^k \\
\end{align*}
$$

Since $x$ is approaching $\infty$, when we swap out the variables of the limit, $y$ is also approaching $\infty$ because $x$ and $y$ are proportional. Consequently, the above proof is possible. Additionally, sligthly altering the limit will yield us another neat limit. Let us define another variable $z$ such that $z = \frac{1}{x}$, which means that $z$ is inversely proportional to $x$. In this case, instead of approaching infinity, $z$ is approaching $0$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty}\left ( 1 + \frac{k}{x} \right) ^ x
&= \lim\limits_{z\rightarrow 0} \left ( 1 + z \right) ^ \frac{k}{z} \\
&= e^k \\
\end{align*}
$$

# Limit of Exponential Function: Example

<Example>
Evaluate the following limits:
1. $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left ( 1 + \frac{2}{x} \right ) ^ {5x} = ...$
2. $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left ( 1 - \frac{1}{x} \right ) ^ {3x} = ...$
3. $\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \left ( 2x^2 + 1 \right ) ^ {\frac{1}{x^2}} = ...$
</Example>

**Limit 1**

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left ( 1 + \frac{2}{x} \right ) ^ {5x} 
&= \left ( \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left [ 1 + \frac{2}{x} \right ] ^ x \right) ^ 5\\
&= \left ( e^2 \right) ^ 5\\
&= e^{10}
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 2**

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left ( 1 - \frac{1}{x} \right ) ^ { 3x }
&= \left ( \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left [ 1 - \frac{1}{x} \right ] ^ x \right) ^ 3\\
&= \left ( \lim\limits_{x\rightarrow \infty} \left [ 1 + \frac{(-1)}{x} \right ] ^ x \right) ^ 3\\
&= \left ( e^{-1} \right) ^ 3\\
&= e^{-3}
\end{align*}
$$

**Limit 3**  
Let $y=2x^2$. Since $y$ is proportional to $x$, the limit variable will also approach $0$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\lim\limits_{x\rightarrow 0} \left ( 2x^2 + 1 \right ) ^ {\frac{1}{x^2}} 
&= \lim\limits_{y\rightarrow 0} \left ( y + 1 \right ) ^ {\frac{2}{y}} \\
&= \left ( \lim\limits_{y\rightarrow  0} \left [ y + 1\right ] ^ {\frac{1}{y}} \right) ^ 2\\
&= e^2
\end{align*}
$$
